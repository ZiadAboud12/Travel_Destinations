The Egyptian Museum

Its History

The Egyptian Museum in Tahrir Square, Cairo, was established in 1902, designed by French architect Marcel Dourgnon. It became one of the first and largest museums dedicated solely to ancient Egyptian antiquities, symbolizing Egypt's commitment to preserving its cultural heritage. The museum's neoclassical architecture made it an iconic landmark, allowing it to showcase invaluable artifacts that had previously been vulnerable to theft and foreign collectors.

Housing over 120,000 artifacts, the museum is renowned for its extensive collections, including the treasures of King Tutankhamun, such as his famous gold mask, and the mummies of notable pharaohs like Ramses II. The museum has played a pivotal role in educating both the public and scholars about ancient Egyptian society, religion, and daily life, making it a significant cultural institution for tourists and researchers alike.
